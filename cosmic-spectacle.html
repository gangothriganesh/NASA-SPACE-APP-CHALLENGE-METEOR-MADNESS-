Journey Through the Meteor Showers

The Perseids

Peak: August

Among the most popular meteor showers, peaking every August when Earth passes through the debris of Comet Swift–Tuttle. They are especially famous for producing up to one hundred meteors per hour, often bright with long glowing tails, which makes them one of the most observed showers across the world.

The Geminids

Peak: December

Light up the sky and are known for their reliability and stunning colors. Unlike most meteor showers that come from comets, the Geminids originate from the asteroid-like object 3200 Phaethon. With as many as 120 to 150 meteors per hour, often in shades of green, yellow, and red, they are one of the richest showers of the year.

The Leonids

Peak: November

Remembered for their rare meteor storms when thousands of meteors streak across the sky in a single night. Caused by the debris of Comet Tempel–Tuttle, they have produced some of history’s most spectacular events, notably in 1833 and 1966.

The Quadrantids

Peak: January

A short but intense shower that appears every January. Originating from the debris of asteroid 2003 EH1, they create exceptionally bright fireballs. However, their peak lasts for only a few hours, making them harder to catch.

The Eta Aquarids

Peak: May

Best seen in early May, come from Halley’s Comet. They are particularly fast-moving meteors, with speeds up to seventy per hour, and are most impressive in the Southern Hemisphere.

The Orionids

Peak: October

Another shower linked to Halley’s Comet, which peak in October. These meteors are known for their speed and the long glowing trails they leave across the autumn night sky.
